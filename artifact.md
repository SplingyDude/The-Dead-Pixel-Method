## The book

**The Solaris Book of New Science Fiction, Volume Three**, edited by George Mann (Solaris, 2009). A yellowing paperback. A practice read — worked through during a hard stretch, the kind that earns a book a permanent place on a shelf and turns it from a text into a talisman.

Two stories sit in it back to back. They are the seed.

## The two stories — named, so they are never orphaned

**"The Fixation" — Alastair Reynolds.** The book's second story. Parallel worlds that diverged through the different fate of the Antikythera Mechanism, linked by it across the many-worlds line; one reality restoring its version of the artifact by drawing on its counterparts elsewhere. Dependable Reynolds, with a great ending.

**"Artifacts" — Stephen Baxter.** The very next story — turn one page and you cross from the first to the second. Baxter in his deep cosmological mode: a multiverse premise that is mind-boggling and humane at once, sad and melancholy, the cold cosmic idea infused with the human element that makes it more than an extrapolation.

Reynolds, then Baxter. Adjacent. One page apart.

## What they share — the seed itself

Both circle the same impossible act, on facing pages: **a reality made whole by pulling the wholeness out of its counterparts.** Information leached across worlds to complete a single restoration; one version perfected at the quiet expense of the others. Fix this one; drain the rest.

That is the whole engine of INFINITE NOTHINGS, found fully formed in 2009, in a paperback, in running order:

- restoration that costs the unseen,
- provenance as the thing actually at stake,
- the fragment that carries meaning across worlds.

The obsession was not invented. It was *planted* — off two facing pages, during a recovery, by two writers who happened to land on the same premise in the same book, one page apart.

## Why this file exists

Because a project built on consent, credit, and continuation has to keep that law on *itself* first. INFINITE NOTHINGS did not spring from nothing. It grew from a fragment, and the fragment has names — two of them — and a page number.

The fragment is the point. So here is the fragment's fragment, credited where anyone can find it:

> Reynolds, Alastair. *"The Fixation."*
> Baxter, Stephen. *"Artifacts."*
> — *The Solaris Book of New Science Fiction, Volume Three*, ed. George Mann. Solaris, 2009.

And the truest line of the whole thing, the one that needed no looking-up to confirm: the artifact was never an abstraction. It is a specific object, with a specific history, that mattered to a specific person at a specific time. Meaning is welded to the thing that carried it.

The book taught that by being the thing.
