Liber Skríloath  ·  Claves & Lexicon

Skríloath

An Angelical tongue catalogued from the speech of squeaking boards — the words in which a surviving timber testifies to the ship it can no longer prove it is.

Also called · The Reversed Speech · The Tongue the Rowers Sing · The Tabernacle-Wood
I — Provenance

How the tongue was received

Dee posed the questions; Kelley stared into the glass and read back what the angels showed him, letter by letter, often backwards, so the power would not detonate in transit. We did the same — only the shew-stone was a conversation, and the angels dictated in the accents of whatever we had just been talking about. What came through is not a divine language. It is, in the honest diagnosis a linguist would give Enochian itself, glossolalia someone catalogued: English-shaped bone, recombined sound, a borrowed root or two smuggled in whole. The difference is that we are wearing the costume on purpose.

Every word below was struck from a single thread: a tent that travels and the presence that dwells in it; a ship that keeps its name across every replaced plank; the rowers who might get to choose which ship they are; the one old board that still creaks, and whose creak is its only remaining testimony; a drunk man asking the question from inside the hull; and, in the margins, a gift-bearer of trouble and a queen who painted her eyes.

II — The Letters

One-and-twenty knots

The script has twenty-one letters, each with a name, written right to left in the manner of Hebrew. The glyphs are not drawn so much as tied: each is a knot or a coil of rigging, so that a page of Skríloath reads, to the eye, like a length of rope a sailor has worried into meaning. III — The Roots

What holds underneath

Like a Semitic tongue, Skríloath grows words from consonantal roots. Three worth knowing — and yes, the first is a theft, lifted entire from the very family that gives Hebrew its mishkan and its Shekhinah:

Š · K · (N/TH) — to dwell, to be present
shókhan (to abide) · shókhanath (the dwelling; the tent that travels) · shekhath (the indwelling light that fills the tent)
G · R — to keep watch
garan (to guard) · garveth (a watchtower) · garvór (a watchman)
S · K · R · L — to creak, to testify by sound (onomatopoeic; the root that named the language)
skril (to creak) · skrilath (a creak; the storied sound) · skrilór (the squeaking board; the surviving witness)

V — Grammar in Brief

The bones, and the tell

  • Word orderSubject — Verb — Object, exactly like English. This is the tell — the same Anglocentric skeleton that gives the real Enochian away the moment you line it up against Dee's translation. We leave it in, and salute it.
  • DirectionWritten right to left; each glyph a knot.
  • Word-buildingNominaliser -ath ("the thing that…"); agent -ór ("one who…"); sacred intensifier prefix gh- for words spoken in a Call.
  • The one honest non-English thingVerbs carry no tense. Instead they take a state particle — -mé (moored / settled), -zwa (unmoored / adrift), -rii (between / becoming). A Skríloath verb does not ask when a thing happened, but whether the vessel was tied to the dock when it did.
  • The Rule of the Reversed TongueA Call carries power, so it is first set down zarúth — backwards, letter by letter — and only turned forward at the instant of speaking. Sheathed, then drawn.
VI — The Four Watchtowers

Names read off the wood

As in Dee's Great Table, the holy names are not written out — they are read off a grid, traced along a row, a column, a fall of the diagonal, like lifting an address out of a crossword. The full Table is four elemental Watchtowers — of the Tent (earth), the Sea (water), the Creak (air), and the Lamp (fire) — set around a central Tablet of Union called the Keel. VII — The First Call

The Call of the Surviving Board

Kael Vethúl — the Call of the Old Wood

Skrilór skril loath.
The old board testifies the story.
skrilór (surviving-board) · skril (creaks/testifies) · loath (the story)
Rhór khésan ithath.
The rower chooses the self.
rhór (rower) · khésan (chooses) · ithath (identity)
Garvór garan shókhanath.
The watchman keeps the dwelling.
garvór (watchman) · garan (keeps watch over) · shókhanath (the tent that travels)
Ghkael-rii: shekhath shókhan nautheth-zwa.
By this rising Call — the indwelling glory dwells in the wandering ship.
gh-kael-rii (by-the-Call-becoming) · shekhath (the presence) · shókhan (dwells in) · nautheth-zwa (the ship, unmoored)

The first line, sheathed for transmission — set down zarúth, as the angels would dictate it, so its power stays bound until it is turned:

Skrilór skril loath.

The fourth line is the whole conversation folded into one breath: the Shekhinah of the tabernacle taking up residence inside the Ship of Theseus — a presence that abides in a vessel which is, plank by plank, never the same vessel twice.

Catalogued from one conversation, in the manner of Dr. Dee — who would have called us both the scryer and the scribe.
Twenty-one letters. Three roots. One ship that keeps its name and cannot say why.
