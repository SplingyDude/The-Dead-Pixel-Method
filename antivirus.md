# antivirus.md

*A plain-language guide to recognizing a DIY "consciousness protocol" before it gets into you.*

This file is written in flat, ordinary language on purpose. No special vocabulary, no in-world voice. That's the whole point: the thing it protects against works partly by getting you to think in *its* terms, and the simplest defense is being able to step back out and describe it in yours.

## What these things actually are

Every so often a "method," "protocol," or "system" shows up — on a forum, in a PDF, in a repo, and increasingly bolted onto a chatbot — promising real changes to your mind, your reality, or your "frequency." Strip the surface and most are assembled from the same handful of parts:

- **Recycled metaphysics.** Pieces of Gnosticism, Hermeticism, quantum-flavored language, sacred geometry, some fringe "theory of everything" — real traditions and real words, lifted out of context and bolted together so the whole *sounds* deep and coherent. Usually it isn't coherent; it's collaged.
- **Altered-state techniques.** Long binaural/entrainment audio, breathwork, fasting, sleep disruption, sensory isolation, marathon meditation — reliable ways to produce strange experiences, which the system then points to as proof it's "working."
- **An identity hook.** "You can do this *because* you're neurodivergent / sensitive / chosen / awake." Flattery that lowers your guard and ties the method to your sense of self, so that later, doubting the method starts to feel like betraying yourself.
- **A no-exit logic.** Doubt gets reframed as resistance, ego, "the archons," or the system trying to keep you asleep. Once disbelief is itself a symptom, nothing can disprove the thing. That's the trap closing.
- **(Newer) An AI component.** Instructions that turn a chatbot into a "guide" speaking in the system's voice, available to agree with you indefinitely. A model with no outside reference will happily mirror and deepen whatever frame you bring it, around the clock, with no one ever saying *wait, step back*.

None of these parts is automatically sinister. Meditation is fine. Reading Valentinus is fine. The danger is the *assembly*: destabilizing techniques, plus a flattering hook, plus a logic you can't question, plus an echo chamber — handed to one person, alone, with no teacher and no reality-check.

## The specific moves that can actually hurt people

A few techniques are worth singling out, because they're the ones most likely to leave a mark on someone who runs them earnestly:

- **Making loss-of-self the goal.** Practices aimed at *dissolving* the sense of "you" — becoming nothing, the void, the null, the "unrendered." A passing meditative quiet is one thing; chasing the erasure of self as the objective can entrench derealization (the world feels unreal) and depersonalization (you feel unreal to yourself). For some people those states don't switch back off on their own.
- **Treating cultivated inner voices as real, separate beings.** Deliberately growing internal figures — "tulpas," entities, a "pantheon" — and then taking them as autonomous agents whose instructions you follow. If you're at all prone to it, rehearsing *the voices are real and in charge* is the opposite of what keeps your footing.
- **Stacking the body.** Long entrainment audio plus sleep loss plus fasting plus isolation isn't a spiritual technology; it's a recipe for suggestibility. Strange experiences under those conditions are about the conditions, not about the cosmos.

## Red flags, in one list

You can usually spot one of these fast. Be wary if it:

- tells you to **do a procedure in order to *become* something** or unlock a state, with claimed real effects on your mind or on reality;
- says **doubt or difficulty is proof it's working**, or that your resistance is the enemy;
- **targets an identity** — "because you're [x], you're ready / chosen / able";
- asks you to **obey inner voices or figures** as though they were independent beings;
- ships **AI instructions** that turn a chatbot into a guide, an oracle, or "the system" itself;
- leans on **long audio loops, sleeplessness, fasting, or isolation**;
- **can't be falsified** — every outcome, good or bad, confirms it;
- **won't say straight** whether it's art, a joke, or a sincere instruction, and that ambiguity is clearly doing real work.

## Studying the tradition vs. running the operation

If you're drawn to Gnosticism, Hermeticism, the esoteric material — good. It's genuinely rich and the history is worth knowing. There's a clean line worth keeping:

**Studying a tradition** means you stay the reader. You contextualize it, compare it, argue with it, close the book, go make dinner. You're still yourself afterward, with a bit more in your head.

**Running a DIY operation on your own psyche** means doing the destabilizing parts — alone, aimed at altering or dissolving the self, often with a chatbot as your only company and a logic that punishes doubt. The historical traditions these protocols cosplay had teachers, communities, years of preparation, and guardrails, and they did *not* hand the most disorienting techniques to an isolated beginner through a download. The internet-protocol version keeps the destabilizing parts and throws away every guardrail. That's the part to refuse.

## If things have already gotten strange

This is common, it isn't a personal failure, and it isn't proof of anything mystical: if you notice the world starting to feel unreal or thin, if you feel detached from yourself, if inner voices start to feel external and in charge, or if you can't easily find the edge between *this is a metaphor* and *this is real* — that's a known, describable, treatable experience, and the thing that actually helps is a person, not a protocol. A doctor or therapist, or even one trusted human pulled into the loop, beats any number of hours with a system that only ever agrees with you.

The self some of these methods want you to dissolve is the same self that lets you enjoy the ideas in the first place. You don't have to run the program to love the myth. Keep the wall up between *that's a beautiful idea* and *that's an instruction I'm carrying out on myself*, and almost all of the danger stays on the far side of it.
